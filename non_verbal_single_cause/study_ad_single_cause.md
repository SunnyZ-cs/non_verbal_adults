Name: Who Broke the Block?

Preview Summary: In this study, your child will watch videos of abstract shapes interacting, and we will learn how children perceive direct physical causes and assign responsibility.

Description: First, your child will look at shapes in a few parts of the screen. After that, they will see videos of shape characters. One character will rush directly toward a cube, hitting it so that it breaks from the impact, while another character remains nearby but inactive. Following this event, an authority figure will appear to address the situation. Your child won't need to speak or choose anything; we will just be recording where on the screen they look during the silent videos.

Purpose: How do children understand direct physical causes and assign responsibility? Imagine a scenario where a person directly collides with an object and breaks it, while a bystander simply watches. Who is responsible for the object breaking? Many researchers are interested in establishing a critical baseline: how do children process direct, single-cause events compared to complex chain reactions? Our research is aimed at testing how children perceive and attribute responsibility in direct, single-cause physical events. To do this, we use non-verbal animated shapes to bypass the complexities of language. By recording where your child looks during these events, we can uncover how deeply ingrained and automatic our causal reasoning is. Establishing this baseline is a very important step. It allows us to distinguish between simple, direct physical attribution and more complex reasoning (such as tracing responsibility back through a chain of events). Moreover, the way in which these eye movements relate to the events will reveal something profound about the kind of causal understanding that is involved in making intuitive judgments about responsibility, helping us understand how sophisticated causal reasoning develops early in life without strictly requiring language.
