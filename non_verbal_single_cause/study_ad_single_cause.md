# Study Ad: Whose fault is it? (Single-Cause Baseline)

**Name:** Whose fault is it?

**Preview Summary:** 
In this study, your child will watch videos of abstract shapes interacting, and we will learn how children perceive physical interactions and assign responsibility when one agent is inactive.

**Description:** 
First, your child will look at shapes in a few parts of the screen. After that, they will see videos of shape characters. One character will stand completely still while another character directly hits a cube, which will break from the impact. Following this event, an authority figure will appear to address the situation. Your child won't need to speak or choose anything; we will just be recording where on the screen they look during the silent videos.

**Purpose:** 
How do children understand physical interactions and assign responsibility when one agent is inactive? Imagine a scenario where one person stands still, and a second person directly walks into an object and breaks it. Whose fault is it that the object broke? 

This single-cause study serves as a critical baseline: we want to establish how children perceive responsibility when there is only a direct physical cause and an inactive bystander present. Our research is aimed at testing how children evaluate responsibility and expectations in simple, direct causal events compared to complex chain reactions. To do this, we use non-verbal animated shapes to bypass the complexities of language. 

By recording where your child looks during these events, we can uncover how deeply ingrained and automatic our causal reasoning is. If we find that children naturally focus on the direct collider even when an inactive bystander is present, this baseline will help us understand how sophisticated children's intuitive judgments about responsibility are when we scale to multi-agent scenarios. As one example, it helps establish whether the presence of other shapes inherently distracts children or if they can isolate the physical actor. Moreover, the way in which these eye movements relate to the events will reveal something profound about the kind of causal understanding that is involved in making intuitive judgments about responsibility.
